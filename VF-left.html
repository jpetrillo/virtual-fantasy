I scavenge and collage to unpack and understand the world around me.

I rummage through simulated alleyways, gathering still and moving images to try and find meaning in the trash.

I’m drawn to the blurred spaces between realities and fantasies.

I lean into sickly sappiness, while pulling away with detached cynicism — weaving together camp and melodrama, constantly shifting back and forth.

I immerse myself in digital landscapes, escaping into these spaces, I begin to desperately navigate through pixels and polygons to find deeper truths in the tangible world that surrounds us.

I take my findings and throw them in a blender, defamiliarizing the content that bombards us daily.

I take my mixture and hold it to a funhouse mirror — covered in vaseline and glitter — presenting a distorted reflection.

The screen is a blank canvas — an infinite universe with limitless possibilities. The warm glow of the screen washes over me, it feels comforting to remove myself from my material form.

But the screen threatens to consume me, I constantly have to remind myself to keep my distance.

Our current technological landscape threatens to engulf us, the new oligarchs of Silicon Valley are exploiting our thoughts, conversations, and bodies to merge our reality with their branded worlds — manipulating our agency for profit.

Virtual Fantasy is a proposition in opposition to virtual reality.

Fantasy is essential to queer survival — we daydream to escape the hetero-normative structures we’ve been forced to live in — imagining new futures without binaries, linearity, or traditional notions of success.

By exploring immersion, escapism, and agency in our virtual worlds through a queer lens, VF offers a reframing of our relationship to VR — embracing digital spaces and their potential to imagine new futures and possibilities, while remaining present in the world around us, as we are here in our bodies, now.

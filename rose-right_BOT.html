My fascination with romance reality television is complex and confusing, I consume both sincerely and ironically. I refuse to consider these narratives “guilty pleasures”, but I recognize the sparkling facade of authenticity.

I do not care if these narratives are true or false. My fascination comes from the detachment of identity these actors practice—selling their person-hood as an authentic citizen and existing in a blurred state of reality.

A reality personality is simultaneously a public figure and a stage actor; they relinquish control of the narrative of their life.

I am fascinated by the birth of reality television and its attempts at documenting the every day while simultaneously incorporating fabricated narratives to avoid the mundane.

Reality television existed in a state of and/or, both real and fake; I watch closely hoping to categorize these moments.

I use the act of screenshotting as a method of subconsciously archiving and analyzing, attracted to both visual and verbal moments that feel strange and/or ridiculous, yet somehow poetic.

I gather these moments intentionally, rewinding over and over with fingers hovering over “command + shift + 3”, waiting patiently to capture the perfect moment.

I become more curious and ambitious, I begin recording and dissecting video footage. I collage these pieces as an attempt to unpack why I’m drawn to this genre.

As an important documentation of American culture, I choose to study the first episodes of two shows — ABC’s The Bachelorette and Bravo’s Boy Meets Boy— both initially airing in early 2003.

I build a shrine for my shows as an exercise in worship.

I kneel at my altar and reflect.

I am a child of the information age, bombarded by content.

My developing brain subconsciously formed associations of the world through the lens of media.

I did not see queer romance on mainstream television.

I passively relied on the unintentional homo-eroticism17 in these hyper-traditional shows to understand sexuality and affection.

My relationship with love and sex is complex and confusing, I am relationship adverse but voyeuristically drawn to romances—both fictional and real.

I peer into these worlds dumbfounded by the sickly sweet sincerity.

I seek validation from male attention; I crave to be desired, using and discarding the bodies of men to confirm my self worth.

I act as a self-destructive fruit, sacrificing safety for anonymity and transience.

I do not believe I deserve to be treated with tenderness.

I cringe at the idea of desiring love.

At times, I do not care who I share a bed with. I need to be reminded I am good—that I am valid—that I deserve to be wanted and consumed.

This is normal. This is good. This is gay culture.

I try to convince myself that this is true26, but I feel empty.

I am an object27 to be consumed and discarded.

My fascination with romance reality television is complex and confusing, I watch the sickly sweetness with a detached irony.

I envy the uncomplicated relationship with intimacy that these actors project.
